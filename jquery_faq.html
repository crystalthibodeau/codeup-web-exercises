National Park Facts

Hot Springs National Park The thermal hot springs waters in this park heat naturally to 143ºF. Lake Clark National Park Lake Clark, centrally located in Lake Clark National Park, is a 50-mile-long clear, turquoise body of water that is fed by glaciers, waterfalls, rivers, and streams. Mammoth Cave National Park Mammoth Cave National Park is the world's longest known cave system, with more than 400 miles explored. Redwood National Park Redwood National Park is home to the world's tallest tree called Hyperion, which earned recognition from the Guinness Book of World Records and remarkably grows on a hill. Sequoia National Park Providing superpower protection, the bark of a giant sequoia tree may be thickest of any tree on Earth––some outer layers of bark measure over two feet thick at the base, providing protection against extreme elements. Yellowstone National Park Yellowstone sits atop the largest supervolcano in North America. Big Bend National Park Historically, people traveled to the Hot Springs Historic District in Big Bend to heal their ailments. Today, visitors can soak in the 105-degree water that gushes from the old foundation of a bathhouse. Capitol Reef National Park The rocks in Capitol Reef National Park are up to 270 million years old. Channel Islands National Park Divers and snorkelers can explore the watery remains of the Winfield Scott shipwreck. Between 1850 and 1900, at least 33 ships were wrecked in the Santa Barbara Channel. Great Smoky Mountains National Park This park is home to over 1,500 types of flowering plants that bloom year-round. A Spring Wildflower Pilgrimage takes places each year, a festival with many guided walks and hikes.
